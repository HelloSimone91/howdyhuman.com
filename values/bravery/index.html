Bravery

Category: Core Values

Facing fears and challenges with courage, even when feeling scared or uncertain.

Why this matters

Bravery turns "Facing fears and challenges with courage, even when feeling scared or uncertain" into a lived value by giving it a form other people can notice. As a core value, it works like an inner standard for repeated choices. It often shows up through the willingness to face before the situation is neat or fully rewarded. Without that behavioral edge, bravery can stay vague even when the word feels important.

Examples in action

  • The child demonstrated bravery by giving his speech to the entire school despite his fear of public speaking.
  • When the moment is messy, bravery may look like choosing to face before the perfect language, timing, or permission arrives.
  • Bravery can be practiced quietly, too, through the choice to confront without turning the action into proof of identity.

How to practice

  • Reread the opening example and name the exact behavior that made bravery visible.
  • Turn the definition into a sentence that starts with "Today I will..." and includes one concrete behavior.
  • Make the value visible to one other person through a choice they could actually notice.
  • After acting, ask whether someone else could have observed bravery in what you actually did.

Frequently asked questions

What does bravery mean as a value?
Bravery becomes a value when Facing fears and challenges with courage, even when feeling scared or uncertain starts influencing what someone chooses, refuses, repairs, or repeats.
How do I practice bravery?
Choose a situation where the value has been mostly theoretical. Then make it visible through one action connected to face, confront, withstand, and endure.
What values are related to bravery?
Bravery often connects with Challenge, Courage, and Patience. Those values can support bravery, sharpen its meaning, or reveal a tension worth noticing.
